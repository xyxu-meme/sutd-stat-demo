The Continuity Correction: Why the 0.5?

Where the ±0.5 in a normal-to-binomial approximation actually comes from.

Background: normal approximation to the binomial

Suppose X ~ binomial(n, p), where n is large. Recall E(X) = np and Var(X) = np(1−p). Let Y ~ N(np, np(1−p)). Then X ≈ Y, and the approximation is particularly accurate when p is close to 1/2.

But X is discrete and Y is continuous. In the pmf of X, each bar is centred at an integer and has width 1 — the bar at k covers the strip from k−0.5 to k+0.5. When we replace that bar with area under the smooth curve of Y, we should match the same strip, not just the point k. That shift by 0.5 is the continuity correction. Written as a formula involving cdf’s, for any integer k between 0 and n:

P(X≤k)≈Φ ⁣(k+0.5−npnp(1−p))P(X \le k) \approx \Phi\!\left(\dfrac{k + 0.5 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)P(X≤k)≈Φ(np(1−p)​k+0.5−np​) P(X≥k)≈1−Φ ⁣(k−0.5−npnp(1−p))P(X \ge k) \approx 1 - \Phi\!\left(\dfrac{k - 0.5 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)P(X≥k)≈1−Φ(np(1−p)​k−0.5−np​)

This is the actual reason for the ±0.5: the rectangle for P(X = k) occupies the strip [k−0.5, k+0.5]. Its area is approximated by the area under the bell curve over that same strip — not by the curve’s height at the single point k.

Set a cutoff k, and choose whether you want P(X ≤ k) or P(X ≥ k). For P(X ≤ k), we sum the area of all the bars from 0 up to k; because the bar at k spans from k−0.5 to k+0.5, that cumulative area really ends at k+0.5, not at k. For P(X ≥ k), we sum the bars from k up to n; the bar at k begins at k−0.5, so the cumulative area really starts at k−0.5. Toggle “No correction” vs. “With correction” to see the shaded region — and the approximation error — change.

Amber bars = the bars being summed. Grey bars = excluded. Sage shaded region = the normal-curve area being used as the approximation; the dashed boundary line marks its inner edge. Watch the boundary line shift by 0.5 when you toggle correction on or off, and watch the error shrink correspondingly.
