The Normal Random Variable

One of the most important distributions in probability: the bell curve. Work through the same storyline as class: start with the universal standard normal, stretch and shift it to model real quantities, then use standardization and tables to compute probabilities.

Why the normal distribution?

The normal distribution (also called the Gaussian distribution, or informally the bell curve) shows up almost everywhere. Many real-world quantities are approximately normally distributed:

  • Physical processes ŌĆö measurement errors, white noise, the velocity of gas molecules in a given direction, tensile strength of alloys from the same production line.
  • Biological attributes ŌĆö human height, weight, and blood pressure within a homogeneous population.

The normal distribution also has many elegant mathematical properties, and it appears in the central limit theorem, which you'll meet in Week 10.

Next: we start with one reference curve, the standard normal. All later normal calculations reduce to it.
